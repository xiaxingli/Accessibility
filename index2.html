BRIGHTNESS

Brightness accessibility can refer to features that help adjust screen brightness and color to improve visibility and reduce eye strain:

Auto-brightness: Automatically adjusts the screen brightness based on the lighting conditions.
Night Shift: Shifts the display's color to a warmer spectrum at night to make it easier to read.
White Point: Reduces the display's white point.

MAGNIFICATION

Screen magnification is an accessibility feature that helps people with vision-related disabilities see content on a screen more easily. It's available on many devices, including Android, Windows, and Mac, and can be used to zoom in on text, graphics, and buttons

CONTRAST

Color is one of the most important elements of a webpage. It can attract users and assist in conveying information. Ensuring websites have high enough contrast between the foreground and the background is significant. Users with color vision impairments or low visibility may find difficulty in viewing an item with low color contrast.

Sufficient color contrast should be provided (at least 4.5:1 ratio) for regular text. If the text is large, a color contrast ratio of 3:1 can be provided. Black text on a white background has the highest readability. Warm and cold colors make for the best color contrast. Use color palettes to find correct color contrast combinations. Viget provides users with a pallet of different types of tints, tones, and shades.

FONT

While there's no fixed ADA font size requirement, accessible ADA compliant fonts are clear, clean, and distinguishable as opposed to cursive or handwriting styles.

The ADA compliant font list includes, but is not limited to: Arial, Calibri, Century Gothic, Helvetica, Tahoma, and Verdana.

Other ADA accessible font factors to consider: It’s generally best not to go below 12px with 16px as a starting point / It performs well when it’s small or large / It has a large x-height / The character is large for its point size / The metrics (such as x-height) are consistent between letterforms / Individual letterforms are distinct in shape and unambiguous

MOTION BLUR

Motion blur, while often used for aesthetic purposes in games and design, can be a significant accessibility concern, particularly for people experiencing motion sickness, as it can exacerbate dizziness and discomfort; therefore, most modern games and interfaces offer the option to disable or adjust the level of motion blur within accessibility settings, allowing users to customize the visual experience based on their needs.

Motion blur can trigger nausea and discomfort in individuals sensitive to visual movement, especially when present in fast-paced games or animations. Providing users with the ability to customize the level of motion blur is crucial for inclusivity and ensuring a comfortable experience.

ALT TEXT

Alt text, or alternative text, is a written description of an image. It allows people with visual impairments to understand the content of images using screen readers. It also makes content accessible to people who have difficulty understanding visual content or who don't have access to high-speed internet.

Here are some tips for writing effective alt text: Be accurate and give a good understanding of what's going on Be short and sweet Include context Include text that's part of the image Keep it free of spelling and grammar mistakes Avoid including "image of," "picture of," or "graphic of"

INSTRUCTIONS

Instructions are helpful tools to improve accessibility, especially for webforms. People with disabilities may not be able to fill out, understand, and accurately submit forms without things like: Labels that screen readers can convey to their users (such as text that reads “credit card number” where that number should be entered); Clear instructions; and Error indicators (such as alerts telling the user a form field is missing or incorrect).
